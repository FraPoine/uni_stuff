In **CMOS** technology the power consumption is due to 
- **Dynamic**
	- related to charging / discharging of logic gates during switching and transistor short-circuits during switching

- **Static**
	- leakage currents in transistors

	- Static power will increase at an exponential rate

## COST ESTIMATION
### Trend 
- Device cost tends to decrease over time even without improvements in the basic implementation technology (learning curve)
	- **Better yield**: larger percentage of devices which survive testing
- By increasing volumes, costs decrease (~10% doubling production): 
	- Faster learning curve 
	- Better efficiency 
	- Non-recurrent engineering (NRE) and development costs amortized over many machines
- Commodity market effect 
	- Manufacturing costs and selling prices are close due to large competition

### COST OF AN INTEGRATED CIRCUIT
- ***the cost of an integrated circuit is given by:***
	- *C = (Cdie + Ctesting + Cpackaging) / Y*
	- **Cdie** = is the cost of manufacturing a die (IC without packaging)
		- wafer of silicon
		- is the cost of making the wafer
		- on the cost of the total number of dies per wafer times the Die yeld (before packaging)
		- How many die = 
			- formuletta slide 26, that is 
			- Total number of dies on a round wafer
			- Number of dies close to the periphery of the wafer
			- where **D** is the diameter of the wafer and **Adie** is the area of the die
				- there is a strong dependence of C on **Adie**
		- **Ydie** = 𝑌𝑤𝑎𝑓𝑒𝑟 ⋅ (1 + (𝑁𝑑𝑒𝑓𝑒𝑐𝑡𝑠 ⋅ 𝐴𝑑𝑖𝑒)/𝛼)^−𝛼 
			- **Ywafer** is the wafer yield (today close to 100%) 
			- **Ndefects** is the number of defects of the wafer per unit area (e.g., 0.4 - 0.8 defects/cm^2) 
			- **α** is an empirical value related to the complexity of the manufacturing process (today ~4.0)
	- **Ctesting** = is the cost for testing a die after cutting the wafer of silicon into individual dies
	- **Cpackaging** = is the cost for packaging and then testing the working dies
	- **Y** = is the yield after the final test (percent of working chips)
	-  **Ctesting** and **Cpackaging** are fixed once the **IC** is made
	- They are mostly a function of the complexity of the **IC** and **NOT of the manufacturing process**
	- ***Adie is the only parameter a designer can control.***

### Trend
- Performance evolution of top processors takes advantage of **Moore’s Law** 
	- *Clock frequencies double every 15-18 months* 
	- level of integration double every lines??
- Performance grows much faster 
	- Effective enhancement are applied not only at the technology level (transistor scaling), but also at the architectural level (higher parallelism) 
- Accurate trend estimation hard 
	- **Lack of univocal testing criteria** 
	- **Use of misleading performance metrics** 
- Basic question: *“How much is X faster than Y?"*
### IDEAL PROPERTIES OF A PERFORMANCE METRIC
- **Linearity** 
	- a metric is linear if when the values of the metric differ by a certain factor, also the actual performance of the machines differs by the same factor 
- **Reliability** 
	- a performance metric is reliable if a system X outperforms a system Y when the corresponding values of the metric for both systems indicate that X should outperform Y 
	- information correspond with the reality
	- facts 
- **Repeatability**
	- a performance metric is repeatable if subsequent measurements return compatible results (within a given amount of uncertainty) 
	- like the MBTI and the Animal in you tests
- **Easiness of use** 
	- if a metric is difficult to use, few people will use it. Also, the probability to obtain incorrect results grows 
	- 
- **Consistency** 
	- A consistent metric is one for which the units of the metric and its precise definition are the same across different systems and configurations 
- **Independence** 
	- a performance metric is independent if its definition is univocal (i.e., not affected by custom external influences)

### THE CLOCK RATE
- The clock rate of a CPU is a very misleading metric, introduced by microprocessor companies mostly for marketing reasons
- is a misleading metric, very useful for the market, is
	- Repeatable (it is a constant) 
	- Easy to measure 
	- Consistent 
	- Independent
- BUT is NOT
	- linear 
	- reliable
- The clock rate does not take the amount of computation within a clock cycle into account
- It ignores the interaction of the CPU with the memory and I/O systems
